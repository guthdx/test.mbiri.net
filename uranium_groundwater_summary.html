In parts of South Dakota, some families use private wells to get their drinking water. But some of this water has uranium in it, which can be harmful to people's health. Scientists wanted to find out where the uranium is coming from and how it moves through the underground water (called groundwater).

They tested water from 140 private wells and found that in some areas, uranium levels were too high. In the northeast part of the area, the groundwater has more oxygen, which helps uranium dissolve and move. In the southwest, the water has no oxygen, so uranium often turns into solid and stays in the ground, making the water safer to drink.

They also looked at the "fingerprints" of uranium‚Äîtiny clues from uranium's different forms (isotopes) that tell scientists where it comes from and what happened to it. This helped them see how far uranium traveled in water and where it might go next.

This study helps tribal communities on the Pine Ridge Reservation understand where water is safe to drink and how to protect it in the future.

What's in the Water?

Many families in South Dakota rely on well water, but in some places, that water has uranium in it, and that can be dangerous. Scientists tested water from 140 wells and found that about 5% of them had unsafe levels of uranium. But here's the good news: by studying how uranium moves underground, we can better predict where it's safe to drink. And for the Pine Ridge Reservation, that means clearer paths to safer water.

Uranium's Underground Journey

Did you know that uranium can act like a traveler in water? In some parts of South Dakota, uranium dissolves into groundwater and moves, especially in areas with more oxygen. But in places with no oxygen, it often gets stuck and stays out of the water. By tracing uranium's "fingerprint," scientists are learning where it goes and how to stop it from spreading. This is important for families who use private wells every day.

Protecting Water on Tribal Lands

Groundwater is life! Especially for communities that rely on private wells. On the Pine Ridge Reservation, a team of researchers worked with local partners to track uranium in the water. They used science and community input to map where uranium is likely to be high or low. This research is helping tribes make decisions about water testing, filtration, and long-term protection of their most vital resource: clean water.

Key Takeaways

Scientists tested 140 private wells on the Pine Ridge Reservation and found that approximately 5% had uranium levels above the EPA safety limit. Uranium mobility varies by location: oxygen-rich groundwater in the northeast allows uranium to dissolve and spread, while oxygen-poor conditions in the southwest keep uranium immobilized. Isotope analysis revealed uranium's "fingerprint," helping identify high-risk zones and inform water protection strategies.

Did You Know? Uranium's movement in groundwater is controlled by oxygen levels‚Äîa process called redox chemistry. In oxygen-rich zones, uranium becomes mobile and can contaminate drinking water. In oxygen-poor zones, it precipitates out and remains in the soil, keeping water safer. This research empowers tribal communities to target water testing and treatment efforts where they're needed most.
